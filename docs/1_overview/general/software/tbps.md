# Data Speed Units (Tbps / Gbps)

The "Speedometer of the Internet" ,the specialized units we use to measure how much information can zoom through a cable or a satellite link every single second.

When you see terms like **Tbps** or **Mbps**, you are looking at the "Rate of Flow." Just like we measure water flow in "Gallons per Minute," we measure the internet in "Bits per Second."

## The Hierarchy of Speed

1.  **Kbps (Kilobits):** The "Snail." Old-school dial-up modems. Good for a simple text email.
2.  **Mbps (Megabits):** The "Sedan." Your home Wi-Fi. Good for Netflix and Zoom.
3.  **Gbps (Gigabits):** The "Sports Car." High-end fiber optic internet. You can download an entire movie in a few seconds.
4.  **Tbps (Terabits):** The "Hyperloop." This is **1 trillion bits per second**. This speed is only found in the massive "Backbone" cables that lie at the bottom of the ocean connecting entire continents.

## The Common Mistake: "b" vs "B"
There is a massive difference between a **bit** (little b) and a **Byte** (big B).
*   **1 Byte = 8 bits.**
*   **Transfer Speeds** (how fast internet moves) usually use **bits (Tbps)**.
*   **Storage Sizes** (how big your phone’s memory is) usually use **Bytes (TB)**.
*   *Example:* If a cable has a speed of **1 TBps** (Terabyte per second), it is **8x faster** than a cable with a speed of **1 Tbps** (Terabit per second).

## FAQs

*1. How fast is 1 Tbps really?*
It is **1,000 Gigabits per second**. At this speed, you could download over **25,000 high-definition movies** every single second. 

*2. Where do we use Tbps?*
It is used in **[[supercomputer|Supercomputers]]**, massive data centers (like Google or Amazon), and in **[[ntn|Non-Terrestrial Networks]]** (satellites) that handle the traffic for millions of people at once.
