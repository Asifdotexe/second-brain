# Halo Effect

"The Angel Script"—a cognitive bias where we assume that because a person has one great quality (like being attractive or well-dressed), they must also be great at everything else (like being smart or kind).

**The Halo Effect** is your brain’s "Lazy Autocomplete." Our minds hate complexity. It is much easier to believe that someone is "100% Good" than to realize they are a mix of good and bad traits. So, when we see one positive thing, we put a metaphorical "Halo" around their head and assume they are an angel.

Think of it like **A Movie Hero**:
*   In movies, the "Good Guy" is usually handsome, has a deep voice, is brave, and is also an amazing cook. 
*   In real life, a person can be a total genius at math but also be a rude, messy jerk. 
*   **The Bias:** When you meet a tall, well-spoken person, your brain "fills in the blanks" with positive traits. You think, *"He looks successful, so he must be worth listening to."* You give them the benefit of the doubt that you wouldn't give to someone else.

## The Dark Side: The "Horn" Effect
The reverse is also true. If someone has a trait you dislike (maybe they are messy or have an annoying laugh), you might assume they are also "Incompetent" or "Lazy." This is called the **Horn Effect** (putting devil horns on them). 

## FAQs

*1. Where is this most dangerous?*
**Job Interviews and Politics.** Studies show that taller men are more likely to be hired as CEOs and receive higher salaries, even if they aren't smarter. We often vote for the candidate who "looks the part" of a leader, rather than the one with the best policies.

*2. How do I stop it?*
**Compartmentize.** Force yourself to judge traits individually. If you're hiring someone, don't just say, *"I liked them."* Ask: *"Did they have the technical skill? Did they have the specific experience?"* Don't let their "Halo" (charisma) blind you to their lack of "Skills."
