# Gambler's Fallacy

"The Universe Owes Me"—the mistaken belief that if a random event has happened a lot lately, it is "due" to stop, OR if it hasn't happened in a while, it is "due" to happen next. 

**The Gambler's Fallacy** is why people lose millions in casinos. Our brains are obsessively looking for "Balance" in the universe. We think that if a coin flips Heads 10 times in a row, the universe must "want" to flip Tails next to make things 50/50. But the universe doesn't have a scoreboard. Each flip is a brand new start.

Think of it like **A Memory-Less Coin**:
*   You flip a coin. **Heads. Heads. Heads. Heads. Heads.**
*   You are 100% sure the next flip will be **Tails**. You put all your money on it.
*   **The Reality:** The coin is just a piece of metal. It doesn't remember the last five minutes. It doesn't "know" it just landed on Heads. The chance of the next flip being Tails is still exactly **50%**. 
*   The coin isn't "due" for anything. You are just imagining a pattern where there is only randomness.

## The Monte Carlo Disaster
In 1913, at a casino in Monte Carlo, a roulette ball fell on **Black 26 times in a row**. 
*   After the 10th time, gamblers started betting millions of francs on **Red**, certain that the streak *had* to end. 
*   The streak kept going. The more it stayed Black, the more people bet on Red. 
*   The casino made a fortune because the ball "had no memory" and simply fell where it fell, while the gamblers were betting on a "fairness" that didn't exist.

## FAQs

*1. Does this happen in the Stock Market?*
**Every single day.** Investors see a stock go up for 5 days and think, *"It's due for a crash! I should sell!"* While markets do have trends, they don't crash just because they "Owe" you a red day. 

*2. Is it different from "Regression to the Mean"?*
**Yes.** 
*   **Regression to the Mean:** Is a mathematical fact that over 1,000,000 flips, the score will be very close to 50/50. 
*   **Gambler's Fallacy:** Is the belief that the **next specific flip** will be the one that fixes the average. Statistics works over long periods, not over single moments.
