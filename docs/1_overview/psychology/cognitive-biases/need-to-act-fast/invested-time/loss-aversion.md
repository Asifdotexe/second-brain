# Loss Aversion

Being significantly more devastated about dropping a $20 bill on the sidewalk than you are happy about finding a random $20 bill inside your jacket.

**Loss Aversion** is the cognitive bias where individuals strongly prefer avoiding losses over acquiring equivalent gains.

In evolutionary terms, this makes perfect sense. Failing to acquire a gain (like missing out on an extra patch of berries) was mildly bad, but failing to avoid a loss (like losing your winter food stash to a bear) meant absolute, certain death. Therefore, our psychological "pain" of losing something is mathematically weighted by our brains to be about twice as powerful as the psychological "joy" of gaining that exact same thing.

This means humans are not rational actors when evaluating risk. We will go to irrational extremes and take massive gambles just to prevent the feeling of losing something we already possess. 

## Key Characteristics
*   **Asymmetrical Values:** The pain of losing $\$100$ is roughly twice as intense as the joy of gaining $\$100$.
*   **The Endowment Effect:** Our tendency to overvalue an item simply because we currently own it.
*   **Framing Effects:** The way a risk is presented drastically changes our choices. People heavily prefer a medical procedure with a "90% survival rate" over one with a "10% mortality rate," despite them being mathematically identical.

## FAQs

*1. How does this appear in behavioral economics?*
It is the core concept of Prospect Theory. It explains why investors will stubbornly hold onto plummeting stocks (refusing to accept the "loss" on paper) while selling rising stocks far too early just to lock in a minor gain.

*2. How do corporations use this against me?*
"Free trials." Once you use a streaming service for 30 days, your brain shifts from "I could gain a new service" to "I am going to lose my service." The pain of losing it forces you to pay the subscription fee.
