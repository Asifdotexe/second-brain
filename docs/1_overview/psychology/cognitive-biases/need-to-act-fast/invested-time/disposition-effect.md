# Disposition Effect

"Cashing Out Early" ,the psychological urge to "lock in" small wins quickly while stubbornly refusing to let go of big losses, hoping they will eventually "bounce back."

**The Disposition Effect** is why many people are bad at investing. It’s the result of an ego trap: we want to feel "Right" as fast as possible, and we want to avoid feeling "Wrong" as long as possible. So, we sell our winning stocks too soon to prove we were smart, but we hold onto our losing stocks for years because selling them would be "admitting" we made a mistake.

Think of it like **A Lottery Ticket**:
*   You win $5 on a scratch-off. You immediately run to the store to cash it in because you want the "Win" right now.
*   Meanwhile, you have a pile of 50 losing tickets in your drawer. You refuse to throw them away because "vaguely maybe" one of them has a hidden prize you missed. 
*   **The Reality:** Throwing the losers away makes you feel like a "Loser." Cashing the $5 makes you feel like a "Winner." You choose the tiny ego boost over a smart, clean strategy.

## Key Characteristics
*   **Locking In Gains:** Selling a stock that is doing great because you're afraid the "Win" might disappear.
*   **Denial of Loss:** Holding a sinking ship (like a bad relationship or a dying company) because "realizing" the loss on paper feels like a punch to the gut.

## FAQs

*1. Is it always bad to sell a winner?*
**No.** Taking profits is a legitimate strategy. The **Bias** only happens when you sell a good thing **only** because you want the feeling of a win, despite all the evidence saying it will keep going up.

*2. How do I beat this "Ego Trap"?*
Remove your ego from the equation. Use **Automated Rules**. For example, tell yourself: *"I will sell if this drops 10%, no matter what."* If the computer does it for you, you don't have to "admit" anything ,it was just a rule you followed.
