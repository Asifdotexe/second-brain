# Utilitarianism

"Moral Mathematics"—an ethical system that says the "Right" choice is always the one that results in the **Greatest Happiness for the Greatest Number of People.**

**Utilitarianism** treats morality like a spreadsheet. It doesn't care about your "Intentions" (why you did it) or "Rules" (thou shalt not kill). It only cares about the **Results**. If an action creates more "Units of Happiness" than "Units of Pain," then it is the only moral choice.

Think of it like **The Trolley Problem**:
*   A runaway train is about to hit **5 people** on the main track.
*   You are standing by a lever. If you pull it, the train switches to a side track where it will only hit **1 person**.
*   **The Deontologist ([[deontology|Rule-Follower]]):** Says "Do not pull." Killing is a sin. If you pull the lever, you are a murderer.
*   **The Utilitarian:** Says **"Pull the lever."** 1 death is less suffering than 5 deaths. The math is simple. One life is a small price to pay to save five.

## The Flaw: The "Minority" Problem
Strict Utilitarianism can lead to some very scary places.
*   *Example:* If a crowd of 10,000 people would get massive "Pleasure" from watching one innocent person be executed, a strict Utilitarian mathematician would have to say the execution is "Good" (The happiness of 10,000 outweighs the suffering of 1). 
*   Most modern Utilitarians (like John Stuart Mill) fixed this by saying that "High Quality" happiness (like Freedom and Justice) is much more valuable than "Low Quality" happiness (like being entertained by a crowd).

## FAQs

*1. Who started this?*
*   **Jeremy Bentham:** The founder. He was so dedicated to the "Real World" that he asked for his body to be stuffed and put on display at a university so he could still "attend" meetings after death.
*   **John Stuart Mill:** The man who made it "Human." He argued that it’s better to be a "Dissatisfied Socrates" than a "Satisfied Pig."

*2. How do I use it?*
You use it every time you make a group decision! When you and your friends can't decide on a movie, and you vote so that the most people are happy—that is Utilitarianism in action.
