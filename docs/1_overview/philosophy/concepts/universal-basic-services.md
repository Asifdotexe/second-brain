# Universal Basic Services (UBS)

Instead of giving everyone $1,000 to go buy their own water, food, and shelter on the private market, the government just provides the water, food, and shelter directly.

**Universal Basic Services (UBS)** is a socioeconomic model advocating that essential human needs and services should be provided unconditionally, free at the point of need, to every member of a society. Driven by the philosophy of the "commons," UBS shifts the focus from simply redistributing money (as seen in [[universal-basic-income|UBI]]) to directly democratizing access to the actual resources people need to survive and participate in modern society.

While almost all developed nations already implement UBS to some degree; such as public schooling and universal healthcare; proponents of UBS argue that the definition of a "basic service" must be radically expanded for the 21st century.

## Expanded Services

A modern UBS framework typically argues that a functional, dignified life now requires free public access to:
1.  **Housing:** Addressing the global housing affordability crisis through mass social housing programs.
2.  **Transport:** Making local and regional bus and rail networks entirely fare-free, which simultaneously combats carbon emissions by reducing private car usage.
3.  **Digital Inclusion:** Treating broadband internet infrastructure as a core utility, rather than a luxury, ensuring equitable access to online education, remote work, and telehealth.
4.  **Food Programs:** Expansion of food-banks and public dining concepts to ensure basic caloric and nutritional baselines are met universally.

## UBS vs. UBI

UBS is frequently debated as either an alternative or a complementary system to [[universal-basic-income|Universal Basic Income]].

*   **The Market Argument:** UBI relies on the free market; you give people money and trust that private companies will efficiently sell them the services they need. UBS proponents argue the free market routinely fails to provide affordable housing or healthcare, meaning rent and medical prices will simply inflate to consume whatever UBI is given.
*   **The Efficiency Argument:** A centralized government can negotiate massive economies of scale (e.g., buying medicine in bulk for an entire nation), making it fundamentally cheaper and more efficient to provide the *service* directly rather than taxing people to hand out *money* to buy it linearly.
*   **The Freedom Argument:** Critics of UBS argue it limits personal choice. UBI allows a citizen to decide exactly how they want to allocate their resources (perhaps they value travel over high-speed internet), whereas UBS forces a top-down, standardized allocation of resources that may not fit individual needs.
