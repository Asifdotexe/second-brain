# Whataboutism

"The Counter-Finger-Point"—a communication tactic used to distract from criticism by immediately accusing the other person (or someone else) of something equally bad or worse.

**Whataboutism** is a weaponized version of the **[[tu-quoque|Tu Quoque]]** fallacy. It is designed to shut down a conversation and make everyone feel like a "Sinner," so that no one can be judged. It changes the subject from "Is this action wrong?" to "Is everyone else also doing something wrong?"

Think of it like **A Speeding Ticket**:
*   A police officer pulls you over for doing 90 mph in a school zone. 
*   **The Whataboutism:** You point out the window and shout, *"But what about that guy in the red car? He was going 100 mph! Why aren't you chasing him? This is unfair!"*
*   **The Reality:** That guy might be a worse driver, but his crime doesn't make your speeding "Legal." You still broke the law. The officer's job is to deal with **you** right now.

## Why it is Effective
1.  **Fairness Trigger:** We have a deep psychological need for things to be "Fair." If one person gets punished and another doesn't, we get angry. Whataboutism hijacks this feeling of anger to distract us from the actual crime.
2.  **Moral Exhaustion:** If you can prove that "Everyone is bad," then the audience stops caring about holding anyone accountable.

## FAQs

*1. Is it always a fallacy?*
**Technically, yes.** In logic, an action is either right or wrong on its own. However, in **Law**, it is sometimes relevant to point out "Selective Prosecution" (if a law is only used against one specific group). But in a debate about ethics, saying "They did it too" is never a valid defense.

*2. How do I stop it?*
Be a "Record Player." Say: *"We can talk about that other person’s mistakes in a moment. But right now, we are talking about your specific action. Please address it."* Don't let them move the goalposts.
