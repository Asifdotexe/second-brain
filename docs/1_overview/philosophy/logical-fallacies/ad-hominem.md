# Ad Hominem

"Attacking the Messenger"—a logical fallacy where you ignore someone's argument and instead attack their character, their past, or their personality to make them look wrong.

**Ad Hominem** (Latin for "To the Person") is a defensive move used by people who cannot win an argument with logic. Instead of proving that a statement is false, they try to prove that the **Person** saying it is "bad," hoping that the audience will assume a bad person can't have a good idea.

Think of it like **A Broken Clock**:
*   A clock is rusty, ugly, and made by a terrible company. It hasn't worked in years.
*   However, twice a day, that clock is **perfectly correct**.
*   If you say, *"That clock can't be right because it's ugly and rusty,"* you are committing an **Ad Hominem**. The state of the clock's metal has zero impact on whether the time it is showing is correct.

## Common Examples
*   **The Scientist:** "This data shows the earth is warming." -> **Response:** "Why should we listen to you? You're a jerk who never tips at restaurants!" (His tipping habits don't change the data).
*   **The Politician:** "We should lower taxes to help the poor." -> **Response:** "You only say that because you're rich!" (Even if they are rich, the idea about the tax code might still be a good one).

## FAQs

*1. Is an insult always an Ad Hominem?*
**No.** 
*   **An Insult:** "You are an idiot!" (This is just rude).
*   **Ad Hominem:** "You are wrong **because** you are an idiot." (This is a fallacy because it tries to use the insult as logical proof).

*2. When is character actually relevant?*
Character matters when the topic **is** character. For example, if you are hiring a babysitter, it is perfectly logical to say, *"I don't trust your advice on safety because you have been arrested for negligence."* In that case, their past actions are directly related to the job.
