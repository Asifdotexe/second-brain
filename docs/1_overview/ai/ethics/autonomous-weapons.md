# Lethal Autonomous Weapons Systems (LAWS)

Giving a drone a sniper rifle and permission to pull the trigger without ever asking a human first.

**Lethal Autonomous Weapons Systems (LAWS)**; often controversially dubbed "Slaughterbots"; are military systems capable of independently searching for, identifying, and engaging targets using artificial intelligence and sensor data, entirely devoid of human intervention. 

This marks a significant ethical departure from a traditional "drone strike," where a human pilot sits in a trailer thousands of miles away and manually pushes a button to fire.

## The Escalation Rationale 

Why are militaries developing them? In modern cyber and electronic [[cyber-warfare|warfare]], communication links to remote human pilots are frequently jammed. To remain effective, drones must be able to make decisions locally. Furthermore, in hypersonic combat or drone swarms, human reaction times are simply too slow to compete against algorithmic speed. 

## The Ethical Red Lines

1.  **The Accountability Gap:** If an autonomous drone misidentifies a school bus as an armored personnel carrier and strikes, who committed the war crime? The programmer? The commanding officer? The algorithm? If no human made the decision, traditional laws of armed conflict break down.
2.  **Flash Wars:** If two opposing autonomous systems misinterpret each other's defensive maneuvers as aggressive, they could trigger a rapid, uncontrollable escalation to full conflict before human diplomats can even pick up the phone.
3.  **Meaningful Human Control:** The international consensus among ethicists is that machines should never delegate the decision over human life to code. Life-and-death decisions require compassion, context, and moral reasoning, which an algorithm cannot possess.
