# Scientific Consensus

"The Jury Verdict"—the collective judgment of thousands of experts. It isn't a "Popularity Contest" based on feelings; it is a "Verification Contest" based on who has the hardest evidence.

**Scientific Consensus** is what happens when the smartest people in a field stop arguing about **if** something is true and start studying **how** it works. It represents the best available explanation we have right now. It isn’t "Settled Science" (nothing in science is ever settled forever), but it is the most reliable guide for policy and medicine.

Think of it like **A Global Courtroom**:
*   **The Idea:** A scientist proposes a new theory. This is like a lawyer making an opening statement. 
*   **The Trial:** Thousands of other scientists (The Jury) look at the evidence. They try to "tear it apart." They repeat the experiments in their own labs. 
*   **The Verdict:** If thousands of labs all over the world get the same result and can't prove the theory wrong, they reach a **Consensus**. 
*   **The Power:** To overturn a consensus, you don't need a "Strong Opinion" or a YouTube video. You need **New Evidence** that is so undeniable that it convinces the global jury to change their minds. 

## How to Build a Consensus
1.  **Peer Review:** Experts check your homework for errors.
2.  **Replication:** Other people do exactly what you did to see if it still works.
3.  **Meta-Analysis:** Combining the results of 500 different studies to see the "Big Picture."

## FAQs

*1. Is the consensus always right?*
**No.** We used to have a scientific consensus that the Earth was flat or that the Sun moved around us. However, the consensus is the **most reliable** guide we have at any moment because it is the hardest thing to achieve. It requires the most proof.

*2. Why do I see scientists arguing on the news?*
**Because that's where the progress is.** Scientists usually agree on the "Core" (e.g., "Gravity exists"). They argue about the "Edge" (e.g., "How does gravity work inside a black hole?"). The media focuses on the arguments because it makes for better TV, which can trick you into thinking the whole field is in chaos.
