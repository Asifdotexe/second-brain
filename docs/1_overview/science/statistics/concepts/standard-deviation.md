# Standard Deviation

"The Spread"—the mathematical ruler we use to measure how much a piece of data "Wanders" away from the average.

If you have two pizza places that both claim an **Average Delivery Time of 30 Minutes**, they might seem the same. But **Standard Deviation (SD)** tells you the "Reliability." 
*   **Pizza Place A (Low SD):** Almost every pizza arrives between 28 and 32 minutes. (Very Reliable).
*   **Pizza Place B (High SD):** One pizza arrives in 5 minutes, and the next arrives in 55 minutes. (Very Chaotic).
Even though their "Average" is the same, Pizza Place B is a mess. Standard Deviation measures that "Messiness." 

Think of it like **Dart Throwing**:
*   **Low Standard Deviation:** All your darts are clustered in a tiny circle around the bullseye. Even if you miss the center, you are very close.
*   **High Standard Deviation:** Your darts are scattered all over the board, the wall, and the ceiling. Your "Average" might still be the bullseye, but you are a dangerous player to stand next to.

## The Rule of "Sigma"
In a **[[normal-distribution|Normal Distribution]]** (The Bell Curve), Standard Deviation (often called "Sigma") is the universal ruler:
*   **1 Sigma:** Contains **68%** of everything. This is "Normal."
*   **2 Sigmas:** Contains **95%** of everything. If you are here, you are "Excellent" or "Terrible."
*   **3 Sigmas:** Contains **99.7%** of everything. If you are here, you are a "Miracle" or a "Disaster."

## FAQs

*1. Why does it matter for risk?*
**It measures surprise.** If a stock market has a "High Standard Deviation," it means prices are swinging wildly every day. This is "Volatile." If you want to sleep well at night, you look for investments with a "Low Standard Deviation."

*2. How is it different from "Variance"?*
**Complexity.** Variance is the math you do to find the SD (it involves squaring the numbers). Standard Deviation is the number we actually use to talk to humans because it uses the same "Units" as the data (e.g., Minutes, Dollars, Inches).
